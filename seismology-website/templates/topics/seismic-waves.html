Seismic waves

The energy released from an earthquake, travels throught the inner layers of the earth as seismic waves until they are recorded at receivers at the surface of the earth.

Body waves Surface waves travel time

Intoduction

There are several kinds of seismic waves. The body waves that arrive first in the seismograph and can travel through the medium or inner layers of the earth, consist of the P or compressional and S or shear waves. Another kind of waves are the surface waves. Surface waves travel only on the surface of the earth and decay with depth. One example of these waves are the Love and Rayleigh waves.

Body waves are the first to observe in a seismogram. S waves are slower than the P waves.The velocity of the shear waves is about the 60% of the velocity of the compressional waves. For this reason the first arrivals are always P waves, then the shear waves and lastly the surface waves. This distinction is apparent in the seismogram in relatively high hypocentral distances from the source, where we observe different and steep amplitude changes. The time distance between the P and S wave arrival time, mainly depends on the hypocentral distance. In great distances, the difference is larger and in shallower earthquakes, the two arrivals are very close to each other. Other phases of P and S waves can also be recorded in the seismogram.

Body waves

Body waves, consist of the P and the S waves. The P waves move in the same direction as the wave moves, that is in parallel to the wave propagation. The particle motion is forward and backward or in other words they compress and dilatate. On the other hand, the S waves, propagate perpendicular to the direction of motion. These waves can further be classified into SH and SV wave components, that is whether the propagation happens in the horizontal and perpendicular to the direction of motion, plane or the vertical and perpendicular to the direction of motion, plane, respectively.

P waves

The propagation is parallel to the direction of motion

SH waves

The propagation is perpendicular to the direction of motion on the horizontal plane

SV waves

The propagation is perpendicular to the direction of motion on the vertical plane

Surface waves

Rayleigh waves

The waves move in an elliptical motion producing both horizontal and vertical component of the motion in the direction of propagation

Love waves

The waves move parallel to the Earth's surface and perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation
